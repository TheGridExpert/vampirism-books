Ashes of Past Dawns

by Marcus Grid

I was born in a small village at the very edge of our lands, a place the name of which has long been erased not only from maps, but also from human memory itself. Once, it thrived at the crossroads of trade routes, caravans passed through bearing salt, cloth, and iron. Merchants would often linger in the taverns and inns, yet despite what one might expect, gold never took root in our soil. People lived modestly: some worked the loom, others tilled and sowed the fields.

My family didn't stand out much from the general mass of people. Though my father, as you might guess from my surname "Grid", came from the west. In his golden years he roamed from settlement to settlement with goods slung over his back. That nomadic life ended the night he stopped in one northern village. By candlelight in a church, he saw a maiden of unseen beauty. That was my mother. For her, he stayed, settled, married, and began saving for a house. My mother was a gifted seamstress: her embroidered shirts and garments were even bought in large cities where my father traveled to sell his wares. As if she had a premonition, she began sewing shirts for children well before they were born. First came my older brother, then me, and lastly our younger sister.

But, as is so often the case, the wind of change blew in a different direction. For some time, there had been whispers in the village about a gang of bandits roaming the nearby lands, robbing and murdering the innocent. Sadly, rumor turned into reality: our home was ransacked and burned to the ground. It was a miracle that we survived at all: no one was inside that ill-fated day. All that remained of what was once a warm, lively hearth was a patch of charred earth. There was no choice but to sell it, and the money earned barely covered a flimsy shack and a handful of sheep. Since then, Father had sworn off trading for good – there was neither money nor strength left for it. He turned to herding, while Mother spun thread and sewed clothes from it, just like she used to. As the years passed, my younger brother and two sisters were born. Though I missed our old dear home, there was nothing to be done.

I was around twelve when my older brother left for the city to find work while I was helping my father watch over the flock. Shepherd life held little excitement, mostly just leading the sheep out to pasture and bringing them back again. I began to think I had settled into this new life, but then in just one summer day, like a thunder in the clear sky, everything turned to ash. Father stayed at home to clean the sheepfold, while I, having led the flock to a meadow, as usual, laid down on the grass still wet from the rain. The day was drawing towards evening, a breeze stirred the air. I was just about to get up and head back with the herd when I decided to feel that nice chill a little longer. And so, I fell asleep.

I awoke at dawn – a sheep was licking my cheek with its rough tongue. Realizing I had slept through the entire night, I leapt to my feet. To my surprise, all the sheep were in place. They were huddled tightly together, shifting nervously, as if something had been disturbing them all that time. I counted them – every sheep was in place. That wasn't the only thing that struck me as strange. Not a wolf, not a fox, not a single predator had dared to approach them, even though the forest wasn’t far away. A heavy silence hung over the field, and a strange unease stirred in my chest. It was as if all the animals were afraid of something, but I couldn’t put my finger on it.

Anxiously glancing around, I drove the sheep back home. I wasn’t even thinking about what I would say to my father, how I’d explain nearly losing the flock after falling asleep in the middle of a field. I just wanted to get home. As I approached the house, bathed in the golden light of the rising sun, everything seemed lifeless. Something was wrong. Not a sound – only the same dead silence as out in the steppe. Not a dense one, but there was a thin mist clinging low to the ground, making everything feel even more unsettling.

Putting the sheep in the pen, I walked up to the door. It was swaying on a single hinge, as if someone had broken it down. I froze: right in front of me lay my father. His chest was shredded in several places. In the corner, fallen off the bed, was my mother. In the next room – my brothers and sisters. All dead. For a long time, I couldn’t process what I was seeing. I did not want to accept that it was real. My mind tried to insist it was just a dream, just another nightmare like the ones that I had been having more and more often lately. But the cold, the stench of blood, and the crushing silence proved otherwise.

Having come to my senses, I jumped outside and rushed over to our neighbors. I ran from door to door only to be met with the same gruesome sight: broken doors, mutilated bodies, death. The entire village had been slaughtered. Alone in a village of the dead, I ran, not caring where my feet carried me – just somewhere, anywhere, where there were still living souls. By the time the sun was at its zenith, I reached a town. Words cannot describe the relief I felt when I saw a living guard at the gates. He called out to me, and through tears and with a trembling voice, I told him everything. To my surprise, without further question, the town raised not guards, but a group of vampire hunters. They immediately set out, taking me with them.

It must be said that, even to this day, the steppe remained largely beyond the control of both vampires and hunters. These were open, empty lands, places with nowhere to hide or take cover. For vampires, there was no shelter from the sun, and fewer animals than in the forests. For hunters, the wind-blasted terrain made fortifications difficult, and with their primary enemy rarely venturing there, patrolling the steppes served little purpose. The nearby lands were mostly used by farmers, shepherds, and traders like my father, since trade routes passed not so far away. That was also why hunters occasionally stopped by – but usually just for a night, on their way to larger settlements where, despite the region's peculiarities, their input was still needed.

After arriving, the leader of hunters silently walked through the village, examining empty houses and lifeless bodies. Then he said, "It has happened again. Another village – the same story". I looked at him. The hunter, noticing the horror in my eyes, tried to calm me, though his words only deepened my dread. Those incidents, as he said, had been happening more and more frequently lately.

The hunters believed vampires were the ones behind all of that. This wasn’t in the style of bandits or pillagers, and certainly not wild animals. And yet, even for vampires, this was strange. Usually, they left some survivors in order to turn them and subjugate to their will. The rest were used for blood. Eventually, the conquered village would raise vampire banners and become a stronghold for their kind. But here, it was as if an angel of death had passed through the streets. With no gain, with no purpose, with nothing but the desire to end every last life. The hunters tried to find some pattern in the destruction of villages, but nothing ever came of it.

Something had changed. Not just my life, but myself, something in the depths of my soul. Maybe I had been lucky to escape the blade of that "angel of death", but inside I had still died. From that night onward it was if I had become a different person. I stopped laughing, I stopped crying. Pleasant memories of the past followed me like shadows. Nearly every night I dreamed of my family – scenes from days long gone. Sometimes, I’d just freeze in place, mentally dragged back to that damned night, forced to live it again and again.

After those events, I was taken in by a pastor from a church in the city I had fled to, shaking with terror. I wasn’t the only child to find a home there: the pastor cared for all orphans, whether they had lost their parents to vampire raids or had simply been abandoned to fate. He became like a father to us. At that time, parish schools had only just begun to appear here, offering basic education even to the children of the poor. We were no exception, especially since we lived there. It was the place where I learned to read and write and was taught the foundations of morality and faith. Though I never became a priest, the knowledge I gained proved invaluable.

The pastor taught us a bit of everything, but his main focus was theology and religious rites. I remember he had his own small library – just a few shelves, but even now that would be considered a luxury. Back then, it was a true treasure, both materially and spiritually. He allowed us to borrow books and read them, but only under his supervision, so we wouldn’t accidentally tear the fragile pages. What fascinated me the most were the alchemy books. Centuries ago, when this science first emerged, the church condemned it fiercely, equating it with witchcraft. But over time, things changed. Vampire hunters became the last defense for the faith and shrines. And so, willingly or not, the church found itself under their protection – and with that, alchemy shed its label as a sin. Though many priests still looked down on alchemists with scorn, our pastor was adding new books on the subject to his collection after noticing our interest.

We were mostly fed porridge, chowders, sometimes cooked with the blood left over from slaughtered livestock. It was no feast, but to us, it felt like a piece of culinary art. Years later, as an adult, I tried recreating that taste to remember the old days. But I never managed to: maybe the ingredients were different, or perhaps me.

That's how my young years passed — under the roof of the temple, among books and the toll of bells. I originally wanted to devote my life fully to church work, to go into the priesthood. But after so many years within those walls, I no longer wanted to stay there for the rest of my life, my soul yearned for something else.

Meanwhile, the situation in the world was getting worse and worse. Vampire attacks on human settlements became commonplace, and our town was no exception. Praise be to heaven, and to the hunters and city guards who managed to repel their attacks for many years. But with each passing year, the raids grew more savage, and it became harder for our defenders to keep the enemy beyond the walls. When I was nearly seventeen, an even worse disaster struck the city. In the dead of night, the vampires broke through the defenses and burst inside. They prowled the streets, attacking anyone they saw, while the surviving hunters did everything they could to fight them off.

Our temple stood at the very edge of the city, not far from the gates. The orphanage attached to the church was housed in a separate building nearby. I was sleeping peacefully when I heard the bells of the belfry ringing the alarm. Outside, I could hear clamor and the clang of weapons. They were soon joined by a loud stomping and squeals of people echoing from the church entrance. Its doors swung open with a clatter, and a crowd stormed in. The children sleeping in the neighboring beds shrank against the walls, too terrified to even breathe.

I crept cautiously to a small window, trying to understand what was happening. On the church steps stood several strange figures. Just before they reached the gates, the ones inside managed to slam them shut. One figure, leading the rest, kicked the doors clean off their hinges without hesitation. As the creature crossed the threshold of the sacred place, its skin began to smolder and smoke. It stopped, recoiling, as flaming scraps of flesh sloughed from its body. The leader gave a signal, and the horde of its subordinates surged inside. The vampires pounced on everyone who had sought shelter there. Though they burned more slowly than their master, they still moaned in agony as flames licked their bodies, but it didn’t stop them from killing. The more blood they spilled, the more fiercely they burned, and when the last living soul was gone, their screams faded too. I stood frozen with terror, unsure whether to flee or pray. That first figure began to move slowly in our direction.

Not even a minute passed before someone knocked, no, smashed our door down with a deafening crack. A woman entered the room, her eyes glowing with a searing crimson fire. "What luck…" she purred, licking her lips. "So much sweet young blood under one roof". I tried to dart back, but the only way out was blocked by her. She lunged at me, fangs bared. Before I could even gasp, her teeth sank into my neck. I felt my body go numb, growing weaker by the second, as if my life was going to end at any moment. Suddenly, one of the children hurled a stool at her. The vampire momentarily recoiled for a moment, pulling away from my neck, but almost immediately she bit again, this time in another place, strangely, not drinking a drop. Then, with utter indifference, she tossed me aside to the corner of the room and turned to the others.

I was barely conscious. Both bite wounds still bled, but there was something else, something unseen, slowly creeping through my veins, as though the bite had cursed me from within. Summoning what little strength I had left, I rose to my feet and motioned the others toward the open doorway. Everyone still able to move bolted forward. "Where do you think you’re going?" the vampire hissed, and in the blink of an eye, her silhouette seemed to dissolve into the air. Before I could realize what had happened, she was already in front of the child who had been first to run, clutching a crimson blade in her hands. With a single motion, she drove it through his chest, then swept it wide, gutting half of those who remained.

"Well, there goes my fresh blood," she muttered with a wicked grin. "Looks like I’ll have to bottle it a–". Never finishing the sentence, with a sickening crack and crunch, an axe buried itself in her skull. Her body limply collapsed to the ground. Behind her stood a hunter. He yanked the weapon free, scooped up the barely breathing boy she had bitten, and turned to us. "Move! Now!" Those of us still standing bolted after him. Soon we reached the door of a small annex of one of the buildings.

The hunter gently set the wounded down, then pulled a few vials from his satchel and poured two each down his and my throats. The first tasted like plain milk, though a little sour. The second one was a vivid red tincture, which tasted like booze, but it seemed to dull the pain almost instantly. "Feeling better? Good..." he nodded. "Down to the cellar. It should be safer there". One by one, we descended. The hunter shut the door behind us and told us to stay quiet. In the dark, surrounded by sacks of flour, we barely dared to breathe. Apparently, we were beneath the bakery.

Outside, we could hear stomping, shouting, and every now and then a muffled explosion. Then, for a time, all went still. We began to hope it was over, until I heard someone approaching the door above. I approached closer to the stairs, straining to listen. Then came a sudden slam – someone was trying to break the door down, hurling themselves against it with full force. Realizing we had to leave immediately, we rushed toward a second exit from the cellar, one that led inside the building.

At the time, I didn’t think much of it, but later I remembered: the door leading upstairs had been left open. Strange, considering it should’ve been locked from the outside, it was a bakery, after all. Maybe someone else had hidden there before us and hadn’t made it. By the time of the attack, the night was deep, and it was unlikely that anyone had still been working. I guess I’ll never know for sure.

No sooner had we climbed up than we heard footsteps coming from the stairs behind us. Without hesitation, we scattered, ducking behind shelves stacked with baskets of bread and jars of flour. We didn’t dare make a run for the open door: we were sure there were still vampires prowling outside.

We held our breath. A figure emerged from the basement. A short, gaunt man. His eyes darted wildly around the room, filled with hunger and fury. The air was thick with the mingled stench of blood, probably from the former owners of the bakery, soaking the floors, rot, dust. All mixed into one suffocating stench. The vampire moved forward slowly, sniffing us out. We pressed ourselves into the shadows of the shelving, too afraid to even breathe. He searched every corner, looked behind barrels, passed right by some of us, yet, by some miracle, saw no one. Then he turned toward the staircase to the second floor and began to ascend. Suddenly, more footsteps were heard from the cellar. A few seconds later, the same hunter who had hidden us before stepped into the room. He was clearly looking for us. The vampire didn’t even have time to turn before the hunter drove his axe into its back. We crawled out from our hiding places and followed him.

It seemed that when he had first found us, the battle in this part of the city had still been raging, and it was too dangerous to get us to the central headquarters. But now, after some time had passed, only lone vampires remained, like the one who had nearly caught us.

We finally reached the headquarters. Just outside it, tents made of coarse cloth stretched on stakes had been pulled apart. These are usually used as field infirmaries, but in the city’s real hospitals hadn’t survived, and the wounded had to be treated somehow. Some tents extended right into the ruins of shattered homes. Everything around was frozen. Only occasional moans or screams came from somewhere in the distance.

The wounded – me and the one the vampire had also managed to injure – were taken to one of the sheds. The fabric was stained – whether with blood or medicine, it was hard to tell. The medics who worked for hunters swiftly treated our wounds with something and bandaged us with rough cloths. It burned, it hurt, but we had no choice but to endure.

I sat on the ground, every inch of me aching. Staring into a fire lit nearby, I tried to make sense of everything that had just happened. I silently stared at the nearby fire, watching the flames flicker, trying to comprehend what I had just experienced. One of the field doctors hung a sword over it. At first, I didn't understand why. I remember thinking that the vampires had destroyed all the provision warehouses, and now all that was left to eat was iron. The truth was a little different. The doctor soon took the red-hot blade and carried it to a neighboring tent. A moment later, a scream rang out, raw, filled with agony: someone was undergoing an amputation. the scream came a stifled sob and then silence again – only the crackling of the fire and the monotonous rustle of the wind driving ash across the ground. A few people who had already received aid lay nearby. One was unconscious. Another stared blankly ahead, as if their soul had been wandering far from here. An old man with a bandaged head sitting near me put his hand on the shoulder of the child sitting next to him. He tried to comfort him, though he seemed about to burst into tears himself. I looked out from the tent. Hunters were patrolling the streets, their faces grim, eyes cast downward. Dried leaves, mixed with ash and blood, rustled under their boots.

Even those vampires who had been aimlessly prowling the already cleared areas in search of survivors were beginning to retreat. The sun was slowly rising on the horizon. From the attic window of a house across the street, a bat flew out, making for the fortress walls. But as soon as the first ray of light touched its wings, the creature turned into human form and dropped with a thud on the paving stones. In seconds, fire consumed the body – skin, then bone, turning to ash, which the autumn wind carried away like it was never there. Dawn was breaking through the dove-colored mist drifting through the deserted streets. Light spilled over the rooftops of abandoned homes, glimmered in the dried puddles of blood. It brought no hope – only the grim confirmation that this night had truly happened. That it had been all real. I sat still, watching the people around me – exhausted, wounded, but alive. With great losses, but the people still outlived.

It was a miracle, a God’s gift, that at least some of those with whom I shared the roof of the temple survived. Fate had claimed not just half of the people I had only just begun to call family, but also the man who had been like a father to us – the pastor. Fragments of memory whirled in my mind: the attack of that vampire, the rescue by the hunter whose name I was never destined to know, the faces of those who were dear to me.

Against the backdrop of blazing fires and gloomy dawn, I knew this could not continue like that. If I was meant to live, then I would use my life to end this cycle of death. Back then, I had no idea how hard my path would be. But I have no regrets.
